How Coding Classes Work

Coding Space is a supportive environment where students develop problem solving skills while challenging themselves to build coding projects they are passionate about. All our classes our self-paced, problem-based and project-motivated.

Challenge Projects

Challenge Projects are at the core of the Coding Space education. Projects are fun and engaging games, websites, or apps that students are challenged to figure out how to build.

Unlike virtually all other coding curriculums, Coding Space doesn’t have follow-along tutorials. In fact, challenge projects are “un-tutorials,” because they’re similar to tutorials in that they guide you to students in building something, except that we don’t include any of the actual instructions on how to do anything. Challenge Projects only list the high-level tasks students need to complete the project, but leave out how to do them. Students figure out the rest from there via tinkering, Googling, asking a friend, and iterating towards a solution, while developing deep insights and intuition of high-level concepts.

We do it this way because we believe that teaching should be about providing students with building blocks, a motivating context, then getting out of their way, and being around for questions. In fact, we pride ourselves on not directly teaching students anything. To us, that’d be like giving away the punchline without delivering the joke. (If you thought math was boring in school, that's probably why!)

Creative Projects

After a number of Challenge Projects, students are given the opportunity to participate in Creative Projects. These are open-ended projects that allow students to combine various skills learned throughout a sequence of Challenge Projects.

Students are encouraged to pair up for Creative Projects, even with students not necessarily at their same level. This provides a authentic team setting for students to collaborate and pair-program on real software projects of their creation.

There’s also time in each class for students to present their Creative Projects to the class, giving each student the chance to develop their public speaking and pitching skills.

Expert Mentorship

Fostering non-cognitive skills, like grit and intellectual self-confidence is a delicate balance of targeted encouragement and tough love.

Our teachers are highly trained mentors that are masters in the art of knowing exactly how much help to give a student, when, and how often. Rather than simply giving hints or answers, Coding Space instructors, draw ideas from students through leading questions and modeling strategies on how the student could find the answer for themselves. Would Google be helpful in this scenario? What keywords would you use?
